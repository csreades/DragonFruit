Abstract

To print a 3D model with a 3D SLA printer, support structures are required
for areas such as overhangs and islands. If there is no support structure below
islands, the solidified material will float around in the vat. Further deposition
                                      re-
in the areas will sink as well, resulting in a printing failure. This issue is called
the floating island problem. To resolve this problem, two different approaches
to detect support points have been developed. One is a slice-based approach
that pre-slices the given 3D model and compares all sets of two consecutive
layers; this is computationally inefficient. The other is a mesh-based approach
                           lP
that investigates primitives such as points, edges, and faces and samples the
clusters; this method, however, tends to miss the local minimums and cause the
problem.
   In this paper, we propose a novel method to create support structures. This
           rna


method does not lead to the floating island problem, in contrast to the mesh-
based approach. Besides, this method does not need pre-slice 3D models, nor
does it need to compare slices. It first converts a 3D model into a geometric
representation called a layered depth image (LDI). It then creates a directed
graph from the LDI to describe the delivery path of self-support and identifies
efficient support points to prevent material waste. The structures can be rein-
Jou




forced by considering the volume as well as the geometry of the model. They
are also reinforced by adding connections between anchors to increase stability.

        Over the past two decades, a great deal of interesting research on additive
     manufacturing has been conducted, and nowadays the general public can design
     and fabricate products at home. However, inexperienced users inevitably face
5                                        re-

     difficulties is the automatic generation of support structures.
        For both fused deposition modeling (FDM) where plastic such as ABS or
     PLA is melted and extruded from nozzles, and for stereolithography (SLA)
     where photocurable resins are solidified by laser, support structures are indis-
                              lP
10   pensable in fabricating an object[1]. This is because 3D printing is a manufac-
     turing process in which materials are stacked layer by layer.

      3. Overview

         To create support structures, we first need to find regions to be supported.
                               lP
      The regions are either overhangs or islands. The regions must have a sufficient
      number of anchors to support it. The positions where the anchors meet the
135   model are called support points. To find them, a directed z-LDI that is nei-
      ther mesh nor slice data must be created. Because a directed z-LDI changes
      depending on the printing direction, it must be chosen first. Because the op-
                 rna


      timized direction is beyond our scope, readers should refer to the previous
      research[17, 18, 19].
140      Given a 3D model with a printing direction, a directed z-LDI can be created.
      It consists of a structured collection of points[20]. Each point of a directed z-
      LDI is an intersection between a ray and the model. The ray is cast from the
      center of each pixel in a two-dimensional grid that matches the resolution of a
Jou




      3D printer, as shown in Figure 3. Although the intersections between a ray and
145   the model can be analytically calculated, rendering techniques such as fragment
      linked list[21] or A-buffer[22] can boost the performance.
         Each sample is either an intersection where a ray enters the model or where a
      ray comes from the model. The former is called an entry point, and the latter is

         Most automatic support generation algorithms take into account only the
170   shape of the model, not its weight. This does not matter in bottom-up printing
      but is critical in top-down. Support structures often cannot withstand the

      weight of the model, which results in a drop from the printing bed. Our solution

      of anchors when the weight of the model is greater than the structure can
175   withstand. We utilize the Poisson disk sampling method[23] for these additional
      support points; this method is described in section 6.
         An anchor can be generated by finding the nearest intersection along −Z(0, 0, −1)
                                           re-
      from each support point. If there is no intersection, it is connected to ground.

180   is a truncated cone shape with a large radius to increase the grounding force,

      of connection to the model to make it easier to remove from the finished project.

         To detect support points, we need to search for the most influential point.
      In graph theory and network analysis, the most important node in a graph is




                                               pro
      called the centrality. The expression of most important can be defined in a
240   wide variety of ways, introducing different centralities such as degree centrality,
      closeness centrality, betweenness centrality, and eigencentrality[25]. In partic-
      ular, eigencentrality is a measure indicating that connections to high-scoring
      nodes contribute more to the score of the node than equal connections to lower-
      scoring nodes. That is, if a node is connected to the node with a high score,
245   then the tail has a higher score.
                                           re-
         How to compute the score is formally defined using adjacency matrix. How-
      ever, this is unnecessary for a directed z-LDI. A directed z-LDI inherits a feature
      such that the tail point of a connection exists at an unconditionally lower po-
      sition than the head point. In other words, there never exists a downward
                                lP
250   connection. This means that the lower an entry point is, the higher the score
      will be. This is because, even if a point has a high score, a lower point that has
      a connection to the point eventually has a higher score.
         For this reason, we sort all entry points of a directed z-LDI in increasing
                 rna


      order by height to find the centrality. In the sorted array, we pick the frontmost
255   sample left as floatable. Then, we designate it as a support point and change
      its attribute to not floatable. Next, we inspect the affected neighbors when an
      anchor is built on the new support point and change their attributes to not
      floatable as well. The distance at which a point can influence its neighbors is
      limited by a predefined distance. The distance from the point to the neighbor
Jou




260   is equal to the sum of weight of edges along the shortest path. By changing
      this parameter, support structures can be denser or sparser. Figure 7 shows the

         365   9. Conclusion

                     In this paper, we propose a method to automatically generate support struc-
               tures for a 3D model using a directed z-LDI. Traditionally, the mesh-based ap-
               proaches tend to have floating island problems because it defines support points
               by uniform sampling or region covering. The slice-based approaches require
         370   time to pre-slice a 3D model and to compare all sets of two consecutive layers.
          Jou




               Our method, by contrast, supports all local minimum to avoid floating island
               problems. Additionally, excessive interfaces are not generated because slicing
               planes are taken into account. The self-supported region can be identified with
               the directed z-LDI, which helps to find efficient support points to save material
         375   and reduce labor in post-processing.



                                                           26
                                  Journal Pre-proof




         There are still some limitations to this research. First, the support volume




                                                 of
      may be larger than the tree-shaped structure. This can result in a waste of
      material. Although we have chosen this type of structure to increase stability
      during fabrication, it would be interesting to research the shape of structures




                                              pro
380   that can reduce the amount of material used and increase the robustness. Sec-
      ond, the proposed method considers only the geometry of the model. It does
      not take into account the actual internal stress applied to the support structures
      during fabrication.
         There are other studies to reduce the need of supporting structure such
385   as multi-directional[26] or multi-axis 3D printing technology[27]. When this
                                          re-
      technology becomes more mature, the waste of the material will be minimized.
